The Zomato Way: 5 Radical Truths from the Unconventional Mind of Deepinder Goyal

Deepinder Goyal, the co-founder of the Indian food delivery giant Zomato, has announced his resignation as CEO of the parent company Eternal, effective February 2026. He will transition into the role of Vice Chairman, while Albinder Dhindsa, the current leader of the quick-commerce platform Blinkit, is set to take over as Group CEO.

By Jesus Guzman Feb 02, 2026 • 09:00 AM EST
5 min read

This leadership shift reflects the growing importance of instant delivery services within the company's broader business strategy. Goyal intends to focus his future efforts on high-risk ventures and experimental projects, including Temple, a health-tech wearable, and LAT Aerospace. The sources further outline the history of the firm from its 2008 origins as FoodieBay to its current status as a diversified public enterprise. Despite stepping back from daily operations, Goyal remains a significant stakeholder committed to the long-term success of the Eternal ecosystem.

The Underdog Who Refused to be Ruled

In the childhood bedroom of a boy who was frequently bullied, a poster served as a silent manifesto: "I was born to lead... I was never born to be ruled." This was the internal world of Deepinder Goyal, a "short, dark, stammering kid" who once scored a dismal 42% in school. At the time, his friends' parents warned their children to stay away from the academic failure. Today, that same boy is the architect of Eternal, a high-performance ecosystem housing Zomato and Blinkit that generated $1.1 billion in revenue for the 2025 fiscal year.


Goyal’s rise is not a conventional corporate success story. It is a study in "counter-narratives", leadership principles that defy traditional management theory to thrive in high-growth environments. By leveraging his perceived weaknesses, Goyal has engineered a culture where character is the primary currency. This is how a man who was once ignored mid-sentence built a delivery empire that redefined the Indian consumer landscape.


1. Goals are for the Mediocre: Why Zomato Doesn't Have Targets

In a move that contradicts almost every business school textbook, Goyal has largely eliminated quarterly and annual goals at Zomato. He views traditional targets as a trap that leads to "negotiated mediocrity." In most organizations, leaders spend months negotiating a number they know they can hit, rather than striving for what is actually possible. Goyal argues that once you give someone a goal, they calibrate their effort to that specific number rather than providing their absolute "best effort."


By removing these artificial ceilings, the burden of performance shifts from a spreadsheet to individual character. In this high-trust environment, the focus remains on maximizing the outcome rather than merely meeting a mandate. It forces the organization to operate on the "right kind of effort" rather than sheer, unguided volume. This philosophy ensures that no leader is limited by a number they agreed upon months prior.


"I believe that if I give somebody a goal, they are going to fall 20% short of it. First of all, how do I know that this goal is the right goal? I don’t know. If I give you a goal, you will actually calibrate your effort toward that goal and that may not be the best effort."


2. The "Rock Bottom" Test: Leadership Through Intentional Friction

One of Goyal’s most polarizing tactics is the "Rock Bottom" strategy. He intentionally pushes high-potential employees to their emotional and professional limits to trigger a total loss of self-confidence. Goyal believes that when someone is at their lowest, their "armor" drops and their "portals of learning" finally open. This intentional friction is a calculated investment, reserved only for those he identifies as having "insane potential."

This process serves as the ultimate filter between a "job mindset" and a "founder mindset." Goyal has no interest in those who view Zomato as a 9-to-5 obligation. He demands an obsession with the mission that transcends the traditional employee-employer contract. For Goyal, a startup is an entity that requires a level of commitment that many would find unreasonable.


The Founder Mindset vs. The Job Mindset

According to Goyal, the distinction between a careerist and a true owner is revealed during moments of crisis:

• The Job Mindset: Identifies a system-breaking bug at Friday 6:00 PM and decides it is a Monday morning problem.

• The Founder Mindset: Refuses to go home on a Friday evening until the customer experience is restored, understanding that startups are built on 9-to-5 clocks, but never 9-to-5 attitudes.


3. Leading in the Shadows: The CEO Who Does Town Halls on Slack

Despite his public status, Goyal has not addressed a physical town hall in nearly eight years. His social anxiety and childhood stammering made the traditional "stage-presence" model of leadership an exhausting ordeal. Rather than forcing himself into an extroverted mold, he turned his perceived weakness into a cultural advantage. By shifting communication to "Slack Town Halls," he leaned into his strengths as a writer.

This text-based leadership has created a documentation-heavy culture where every discussion is searchable, thoughtful, and transparent. The irony is not lost on him: a man who was ignored mid-sentence as a child now commands an empire through digital threads. He has reached a state of peace with his condition, recognizing that his achievements have finally earned him the right to be heard.

"Stammering makes me nervous... it’s not easy and comfortable for me to do this. But now I am like, even if I stammer, people will now listen to me. I have earned a little bit of that."


4. Friendship is a Feature, Not a Bug: The $750 Million Blinkit Bet

When Zomato acquired Blinkit (formerly Grofers) for approximately $750 million, the move was dismissed by critics as a "bailout" of a friend. Blinkit’s founder, Albinder Dhindsa, has been a close friend of Goyal's since they were 15. However, Goyal argues that personal trust is a strategic "feature" when betting the company's future on a single move. He describes the acquisition as an emotional and spiritual agreement—a commitment to "go to war hand-in-hand."

This personal bond, however, does not shield a partner from Goyal’s brutal objectivity. Post-acquisition, Goyal asked Dhindsa to leave the organization on two separate occasions because he felt Dhindsa was not yet the "right leader for the transition" from Grofers to the new Blinkit model. This illustrates a rare balance: a trust deep enough to bet the company on, yet a mission-first mentality that demands the right leadership at the right time.

5. Loyalty in Crisis: The 30x Return on Sacrifice

The COVID-19 pandemic provided the definitive test of Zomato's high-trust culture. With revenue effectively wiped out, Goyal asked for voluntary salary cuts—some as high as 100%—without promising equity in exchange. He wanted to see who was truly invested in the survival of the firm. Nearly 80% of the 4,000-person organization volunteered for the cut, proving a level of team-wide commitment rarely seen in corporate India.

This sacrifice was not forgotten, but it was also not part of a pre-negotiated deal. Once Zomato achieved its IPO, the company granted ESOPs to those who had sacrificed as a surprise "thank you." Those who had let go of ₹100 in cash during the crisis saw a 30x return on that value through their equity. By refusing to make it a transactional exchange, Goyal cemented a core team of long-term believers who had proven their loyalty in the dark.

Closing Words: The Power of Childlike Curiosity

Deepinder Goyal insists he does not have a 10-year vision or a rigid MBA-style roadmap. He identifies simply as a "childlike curious person" who wants to solve the problems immediately in front of him. This agility stems from his refusal to be locked into an identity—whether as a "stammerer," a "billionaire," or a "CEO." He advocates for focusing on fundamentals and maintaining a mindset that can pivot as the market shifts.

However, this curiosity is balanced by an intense, underlying pressure. Goyal famously employs the "gun to the head" metaphor for business: "The gun should never go off your head." It is this duality—the wonder of a child and the urgency of a survivor—that defines Zomato’s relentless pace. Success, in Goyal's world, is a byproduct of staying curious while never allowing the urgency of the mission to fade.


Final Thought: If you removed all your quarterly goals tomorrow, would your team work harder—or stop working entirely?
