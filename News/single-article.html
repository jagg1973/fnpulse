Global Markets Rally as Inflation Data Beats Expectations

Stocks surged across Asia and Europe today after fresh data showed inflation cooling faster than anticipated, fueling hopes for early rate cuts by major central banks.

By Sarah Jenkins Senior Markets Reporter Published Jan 21, 2026 • 08:30 AM EST • Dateline London
4 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Global equities rose after softer inflation prints signaled earlier rate cuts.
  • Traders now price a 75% chance of a March Fed cut, up from 40% last week.
  • Tech stocks led gains as bond yields dropped below 3.8%.

LONDON — Global equity markets posted significant gains on Tuesday as the latest consumer price index (CPI) reports from the Eurozone and the United States suggested inflationary pressures are subsiding faster than economists had predicted.

The pan-European Stoxx 600 index climbed 1.2% by mid-afternoon trading, led by technology and consumer discretionary stocks. In Asia, the Nikkei 225 jumped 2.1%, reaching a new three-month high.

300x250 Ad

Central Banks Pivot?

"The data is unequivocal," said Marcus Thorne, Chief Strategist at Meridian Capital. "We are seeing a broad-based disinflationary trend that will force the hand of central bankers sooner rather than later."

Traders are now pricing in a 75% chance of a rate cut by the Federal Reserve in March, up from just 40% a week ago. This shift in sentiment has sent bond yields tumbling, with the US 10-year Treasury yield falling below 3.8%.

"This is the goldilocks scenario investors have been waiting for: resilient growth with falling prices."

Tech Sector Leads Charge

Technology stocks were the biggest beneficiaries of the rate optimism. Semiconductor giants and software firms saw their valuations expand as the cost of capital outlook improved.

  • Nvidia rose 4.5% in pre-market trading.
  • ASML gained 3.2% in Amsterdam.
  • Samsung Electronics closed 2.8% higher in Seoul.

Market analysts warn, however, that while the trend is positive, geopolitical risks remain a wildcard that could disrupt supply chains and reignite inflation fears later in the year.
